# Product

## Register

product

## Users

The primary user is the project owner: a person with diabetes using insulin pump, CGM, nutrition, fitness, and journal data to understand patterns and prepare clearer therapy-team conversations. If the tool proves viable, it may become an open-source, self-hosted dashboard for others who want to do similar personal diabetes data review.

Users are often reviewing dense data in focused sessions, comparing days and summary periods, and looking for recurring patterns rather than one-off conclusions. The interface should support careful inspection, repeat use, and confidence when discussing findings with clinicians.

## Product Purpose

The product combines Tidepool/Twiist pump and CGM exports, journal summaries, meal-window analysis, and future nutrition/fitness telemetry into a local dashboard for pattern discovery.

It exists to help users see where insulin delivery, glucose response, meals, exercise, and daily habits interact. Success means the user can identify recurring time-window patterns, understand pump correction behavior, and bring concrete observations to their therapy team to support better diabetes control, healthier weight goals, and overall health.

## Brand Personality

Clinical, calm, precise, and innovative.

The product should feel trustworthy and data-literate without becoming cold. It should be beautiful and engaging enough that users want to return to their data, but it should never feel like a flashy fitness app, gamified wellness toy, or generic SaaS template. The emotional goal is confident curiosity: users should feel invited to investigate their diabetes, fitness, and nutrition patterns with clarity rather than judgment.

## Anti-references

- Flashy consumer fitness dashboards that overuse badges, streaks, confetti, or motivational noise.
- Dark, high-gloss "command center" dashboards that make health data feel dramatic or intimidating.
- Generic SaaS analytics pages with decorative cards, vague metrics, and weak hierarchy.
- Clinical software that is dense but visually exhausting, hard to scan, or indifferent to the user's emotional load.
- Any design that implies automated medical recommendations instead of pattern review and therapy-team discussion.

## Design Principles

- Make patterns inspectable. The interface should expose how each metric is derived and let users move between summary periods and individual days without losing context.
- Support therapy-team conversations. Charts and tables should turn vague experiences into concrete, time-window-specific observations.
- Engage without gamifying. The product should encourage involvement in diabetes, nutrition, and fitness activity through clarity, beauty, and momentum rather than pressure or reward mechanics.
- Treat trust as a feature. Data provenance, verification state, import progress, and custom metric definitions should be visible when they affect interpretation.
- Prefer calm precision. Visual emphasis should help users compare, rank, and investigate, not decorate the page.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

The dashboard should target WCAG AA contrast for text and controls. Charts should avoid relying on color alone; labels, legends, tooltips, and direct annotations should carry meaning. Motion should be minimal, state-driven, and respect reduced-motion preferences. The interface should remain usable for long review sessions, including users with diabetes-related eye strain, color-vision differences, or fluctuating attention during health management.
